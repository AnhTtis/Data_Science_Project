\begin{abstract}

We compute the differentials of a few elements of Sinha's spectral sequence for cohomology of the space of long knots modulo immersions in codimension one over a field of characteristic $2$ or $3$. We show that $d_2$ of an element is non-zero in characteristic $2$, which has already been proved by Salvatore essentially, and $d_3$ of another element is non-zero in characteristic $3$. While even convergence of the sequence is unclear in condimension one, these results have some application to non-formality of operads. The result in characteristic $3$ implies planar non-formality of the standard map $C_*(E_1)\to C_*(E_2)$ in characteristic $3$, where $C_*(E_k)$ denotes the chain little $k$-disks operad. We also reprove the result of Salvatore which states $C_*(E_2)$ is not formal as a planar operad in characteristic $2$.  For computation, we use a duality between configuration spaces and fat diagonals.   
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A long knot is a smooth embedding $\RR\to \RR^d$ which coincides with a fixed linear embedding  outside of a compact set. Sinha introduced a spectral sequence for cohomology of the space of  long knots based on Goodwillie-Weiss calculus\cite{GK,weiss}, see \cite{sinha, sinha1}. Sinha's spectral sequence is closely related to Vassiliev's spectral sequence  and also related to the little disks operads. Lambrechts-Turchin-Voli\'c \cite{LTV} proved the rational collapse of Vassiliev's sequence for $d\geq 4$ using these relations. For other coefficients, Boavida de Brito-Horel \cite{BH} proved that infinitely many higher differentials of Sinha's sequence vanish in $p$-adic integers.\\
\indent In this paper, we consider Sinha's sequence for $d=2$, the case of {\em codimension one}. While even convergence  is unclear, it is relatively easy to find a new computational example since the rational collapse is not known in this case. We compute   higher differentials of two elements as follows.
\begin{thm}[Theorems \ref{Tdifferential}, \ref{Tch3}]\label{Tmain}
Let $\EE_r^{p,q}$ be Sinha's spectral sequence for  cohomology of the space of long knots modulo immersions in codimension one. More precisely, $\EE^{p,q}_r$ is the Bousfield-Kan type cohomology spectral sequence induced by the cosimplicial space $\KK^\bullet_2$ defined in \cite{sinha1}, which consists of compactified configuration spaces of ordered points in $\RR^2$  (see Definitions \ref{DKontsevich}, \ref{Dspecseq}).

\begin{enumerate}
\item (\cite{salvatore}) In characteristic $2$, there exists an element in $\EE_2^{-4,2}$ which is not annihilated by $d_2$.
\item In characteristic $3$, there exists an element in $\EE_3^{-5,3}$ which is not annihilated by $d_3$. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{thm}
Part 1 (in homological context) has already been proved implicity by Salvatore \cite{salvatore}. Turchin \cite{turchin} introduced a notion of divided product which produces elements of Sinha's (or Vassiliev's) sequence in terms of graphs. The whole of the  products forms an interesting subcomplex of a page  of Sinha's sequence. The elements in parts 1 and 2 of Theorem \ref{Tmain} correspond to the products $\langle Z_1,Z_1\rangle$ and $\langle Z_1, Z_2\rangle$ in the notation of \cite{turchin}, respectively while we use a version reversing the order of vertexes. It might be interesting to compute $d_p$ of $\langle Z_1, Z_{p-1}\rangle$ for other primes $p$.\\
\indent Theorem \ref{Tmain} can be related to formality. Let $f:\mathcal{O}\to \mathcal{P}$ and $f':\mathcal{O}'\to \mathcal{P}'$ be maps of chain operads. A { quasi-isomorphism from $f$ to $f'$} is a commutative square
\[
\xymatrix{\mathcal{O} \ar[r]\ar[d] ^f& \mathcal{O'} \ar[d]^{f'} \\
\mathcal{P}\ar[r]& \mathcal{P'}}
\]
 of maps of operads where the horizontal maps induce quasi-isomorphisms of complexes at each arity. A map $f:\mathcal{O}\to\mathcal{P}$ is said to be { formal} if it is connected with the induced map $H_*(f)$  on  homology by a zigzag of quasi-isomorphisms. A chain operad $\mathcal{O}$ is formal if the map from the initial object to $\mathcal{O}$ is formal.  
Formality of the little disks operads proved in \cite{tamarkin, kontsevich, LV} was essentially used in the proof of rational collapse of Vassiliev's sequence. Since Sinha's sequence comes from a map of operads, Theorem \ref{Tmain} 
has the following  corollary about relative planar non-formality (see \cite{moriya}).
\begin{cor}\label{Cnon-formality_ch3}
Let $E_k$ be the little $k$-disks operad and $E_1\to E_2$  the map induced by the inclusion $\RR\to \RR^2$ to the first coordinate. The induced map $C_*(E_1)\to C_*(E_2)$  between singular chains is not formal as a map of planar (or non-symmetric) operads in characteristic $3$\hfill\qedsymbol
\end{cor}
Using the part 1 of Theorem \ref{Tmain}, we also reprove the (absolute) planar non-formality in characteristic $2$ first proved in \cite{salvatore}.
\begin{cor}[\cite{salvatore}, see section \ref{Sabsolute}]\label{Cnon-formality_ch2}
The chain little $2$-disks operad $C_*(E_2)$ is not formal as a planar  operad  in characteristic $2$.
\end{cor}
As related works, Turchin-Willwacher \cite{TW} proved non-formality of the map $C_*(E_k)\to C_*(E_{k+1})$ over reals for $k\geq 1$. In \cite{livernet}, Livernet proved non-formality of the Swiss-cheese operads. In \cite{moriya2}, non-formality of the framed little disks operads of odd dimension $\geq 5$ was proved.\\ 
\indent Our method of computation is different from \cite{salvatore} which makes use of  obstruction theory and  a combinatorial model of the operad. We use a duality between configuration spaces and fat diagonals, which is similar to the one given in the former paper \cite{moriya1}, to transfer necessary structure to fat diagonals and resolve the structure by $\check{\text{C}}$ech complex. We use chains in this resolution for diagram chasing.  Perhaps, the main feature of this method is that the chains  are labeled by graphs and defined  by relatively simple maps. So we can use graphical and geometric intuition  to some extent. While the computation in this paper is restricted to condimension one, we prepare most of basic notions in  a form applicable to any codimension.
\subsection{Notation and terminology}\label{SSNT}

\begin{enumerate}
\item $\CG$ denotes the category of unpointed compactly generated spaces and continuous maps and $\CG_*$ the corresponding pointed category. All spaces in this paper belong to $\CG$ or $\CG_*$. 

\item For an unpointed (resp. pointed) topological space $X$, $C_*(X)$ (resp. $\bar C_*(X)$) denotes the  normalized singular chain complex (resp. the reduced normalized singular chain complex) with coefficients in a fixed field $\kk$. For chains $a\in \bar C_*(X)$, $b\in \bar C_*(Y)$, $a\wedge b\in \bar C_*(X\wedge Y)$ denotes the Eilenberg-Zilber shuffle product. For the transposition map $T:X\wedge Y\to Y\wedge X$, we have the following identity at chain level:
\[
T_*(a\wedge b)=(-1)^{|a||b|}b\wedge a, 
\]
where $|a|$ denotes the degree of $a$. Hereafter,  for a pointed map $f:X\to Y$, the subscript $*$ for the pushforward on homology is omitted if no confusion occurs.  So $f_*(a)$ is denoted by $f(a)$.

\item Let $X$ be an unpointed space. We denote  $X$ with disjoint basepoint by $X_+$ and the one-point compactification of $X$  by $X^*$. We set  $S^k=(\RR^k)^*$ and $[0,\infty]=[0,\infty)^*$. We denote the interval $[0,1]$ by $I$. We fix a fundamental cycle $w_{S^2}\in \bar C_2(S^2)$, chains $w_{\infty}\in \bar C_1([0,\infty])$ and $w_I\in C_1(I)$ such that $dw_\infty=\{0\}$ and $dw_I=\{1\}-\{0\}$, where $\{0\}, \{1\}$ are cycles represented by $0,1\in [0,1]\subset [0,\infty]$.  We set 
\[
w_k=(w_{S^2})^{\wedge 2}\wedge (w_\infty)^{\wedge k},\qquad  \ w_{k l}=w_k\wedge (w_I)^{\wedge l}\qquad  \text{ for } k\geq 0,\  l>0.
\]
\item $|-|$ denotes the standard Euclidean norm. We define elements $u,v\in \RR^d$ by
\[
u=(1,0,\dots, 0), \text{ \ and \ } v=(0,1,0,\dots, 0).
\]
\item We denote by $=_1$, $<_1$,$\dots$ etc., the relations between the first coordinates of elements of $\RR^k$. For example,  for two elements $x=(x_1,\dots, x_k), y=(y_1,\dots, y_k)\in \RR^k$ and a number $t\in \RR$, $x<_1y$ and $x=_1y$ mean $x_1<y_1$ and $x_1=y_1$, respectively, and  $x<_1 t$ means $x_1< t$.

\end{enumerate}
\section{Punctured knot model  and  configuration space model }\label{SPK}
For some reason, we mainly deal with a version of the punctured knot model defined below instead of the cosimplicial model. 
\begin{defi}\label{Dpartition0}
A {\em partition $P$ of } $[n+1]=\{0,1,\dots, n+1\}$ is a set of subsets of $[n+1]$ satisfying the following conditions.
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\cup_{\alpha \in P}\alpha =[n+1]$.
\item Each element of  $P$ is non-empty.
\item If $\alpha, \beta \in P$, either of $\alpha=\beta$ or $\alpha\cap \beta=\emptyset$ holds.
\item For each element  $\alpha \in P$ and pair of numbers $i, j\in \alpha$ with $i<j$, any number $k$ with $i<k<j$ belongs to $\alpha$. 
\item $\# P\geq 2$, in other words the set consisting of the single element $[n+1]$ is not a partition. 
\end{enumerate} 
We call an element of  $P$ a {\em piece of} $P$. We regard a partition as a totally ordered set via the order induced by $[n+1]$. A partition $Q$ is said to be a {\em subdivision of} $P$ if $Q\not=P$ and each piece of $Q$ is contained in some piece of $P$. $\PP_n$ denotes the poset of partitions of $[n+1]$. Its objects are the partitions. 
A non-identity morphism $P\to Q$ exists if and only if $Q$ is a subdivision of $P$. By abuse of notation, we identify the partition $\{\{0\},\{1\},\dots, \{n+1\}\}$ consisting  of singletons with $[n+1]$.  
\end{defi}
\begin{exa}\label{Epartition}
The following sets are examples of partitions of $[5]$:
\[
P=\{\{0\},\{12\},\{345\}\}, \qquad Q=\{\{0\},\{12\}, \{3\}, \{45\}\}.
\]
We omit commas in pieces, so the piece $\{12\}$ denotes $\{1,2\}$. We see that $\#P=3$, $\#Q=4$, and  $Q$ is a subdivision of $P$.  
\end{exa}



\begin{defi}\label{DPK}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Throughout the paper, we fix positive numbers $\rho, \eps$ and $c_0,\dots c_{n+1}$ satisfying 
\[
c_0+\cdots +c_{n+1}=1, \quad \rho<1,\quad 100\eps/\rho<c_0, \quad 100\bigl(\eps/\rho+\sum_{j<i}c_j\bigr)<c_i \quad (1\leq i\leq n+1).
\] (The last two inequalities are not used until section \ref{Scondensed}.)
\item We define a functor $\PK:\PP_n\to \CG$ as follows.
Set $b_i=c_0+\cdots +c_{i-1}$ for $1\leq i\leq n$. For a partition $P=\{\alpha_0<\cdots <\alpha_{p+1}\}$,  $S_P\subset \{1,\dots, n+1\}$ denotes the set of minimum elements in each of $\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_{p+1}$. $\PK(P)$ is the space of embeddings 
$f:[0,1]-\{b_i\mid i\in S_P\}\to D^d$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
\item $f(0)=-u$ and $f(1)=u$ \\
\item In each connected component of the domain, $f(t)=x+ct u$ for some constant elements $x\in D^d$ and $c>0$. 
\end{enumerate}
For a subdivision $Q$ of $P$, the map $\PK(P)\to \PK(Q)$ is the obvious restriction.
\end{enumerate}
\end{defi}
Let $\Delta_n$ be the category whose objects are $[k]$ ($0\leq k\leq n$) and whose morphisms are the weakly order preserving maps. We define a functor $\mathcal{F}:\PP_n\to \Delta_n$ by $P\mapsto [\# S_P-1]$.
\begin{defi}\label{DKontsevich}
Let $\KK_d$ denotes the $d$-dimensional Kontsevich operad defined in \cite{sinha1}. Its $p$-th term $\KK_d(p)$ is a version of Fulton-Macpherson compactification of the ordered configuration space $Conf_p(\RR^d)$ of $p$ points. The operad $\KK_d$ is equipped with a map $\ASS\to \KK_d$ from the associative operad as in \cite{sinha1}. This map induces a cosimplicial space $\KK_d^\bullet$, which we call {\em Sinha's cosimplicial space}, via the framework of McClure-Smith. We use the same notation for its restriction to $\Delta_n$ if no confusion occurs.
\end{defi}
\begin{lem}\label{LPK}
There is a zigzag of natural homotopy equivalences
\[
\PK\simeq \mathcal{F}^*\KK_d^\bullet.
\]

\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Let  $\Emb_c([0,1], D^d)$ be the space of  embeddings $[0,1]\to D^d$ to the $d$-dimensional unit closed disk with fixed endpoints in $\partial D^d$ and fixed tangent vectors on them.  When we identify $\PP_n$ and the poset of non-empty subsets of $\{1,\dots, n+1\}$ by $P\mapsto S_P$, after some change of parametrization of $[0,1]$, $\PK$ is naturally homotopy equivalent to the homotopy  fiber of the map from the punctured knot model of $\Emb_c([0,1], D^d)$ to the cubical model of $\Imm_c([0,1],D^d)\simeq \Omega S^{d-1}$ given by $P\mapsto  (S^{d-1})^{\#P-2}$.
With this observation, the claim is obvious by the arguments in \cite{sinha, sinha1}.
\end{proof}

\begin{defi}\label{Dpunctured}
\begin{enumerate}
\item 
For $P\in \PP_n$ and $\alpha, \beta \in P$ with $\alpha<\beta$, we set 
\[
\begin{split}
c_\alpha:=& \sum_{i\in \alpha}c_i, \\
c_{\leq \alpha}:= & \sum_{\gamma\in P, \gamma<\alpha}c_\gamma+c_\alpha/2, \\
c_{\geq \alpha}:= & \sum_{\gamma\in P, \gamma>\alpha}c_\gamma+c_\alpha/2, \\
c_{\alpha \beta}:= & \sum_{\gamma\in P, \alpha <\gamma< \beta}c_\gamma+(c_\alpha+c_\beta)/2. 
\end{split}
\]
In fact, this definition does not depend on the pieces of $P$ other than $\alpha, \beta$. We abbreviate $c_{\leq \{i\}}$ (resp. $c_{\geq \{i\}}$) as $c_{\leq i}$ (resp. $c_{\geq i}$).
\item Let $Q$ be a subdivision of $P$ and write $P=\{\alpha_0<\cdots <\alpha_{p+1}\}$ and $Q=\{\beta_0<\cdots <\beta_{q+1}\}$. We define an affine monomorphism $e_{P,Q}:\RR^{dp}\to \RR^{dq}$ as follows. Let $(x_i)_{1\leq i\leq p}\in \RR^{dp}$ be an element. For convenience, we set 
\[
x_0=(-1+\rho c_{\alpha_0}/2,0,\dots, 0),\quad x_{p+1}=(1-\rho c_{\alpha_{p+1}}/2,0,\dots,0).
\] For $0\leq i\leq p+1$, suppose that $\alpha_i$ includes exactly $k$-pieces of $Q$, say $\beta_l,\dots, \beta_{l+k-1}$. We create the line segment which is centered at $x_i$, parallel to $u$, and of length $\rho c_{\alpha_i}$, and divide this segment into $k$ little segments of length $\rho c_{\beta_l},\dots ,\rho c_{\beta_{l+k-1}}$ arranged from left to right. Let $y_{l+j-1}$ be the center of the $j$-th little segment. We set $e_{P,Q}((x_i)_{i})=(y_{m})_{1\leq m\leq q}$. For $Q=[n+1]$  we write $e_{P,Q}=e_P$. It is clear that $e_{Q,R}\circ e_{P,Q}=e_{P,R}$ for a subdivision $R$ of $Q$. 
\item We define a functor $\mathcal{E}:\PP_n\to \CG$ as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
\item For $P\in \PP_n$ with $\# P-2=p$, $\mathcal{E}(P)$ is the subset of $\RR^{dp}$ consisting of elements $(x_\alpha)_{\alpha\in P}$ satisfying the following inequalities:
\[
\begin{split}
|x_\alpha|& \leq 1-\rho c_\alpha/2,\  \text{and} \\
-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha} &\leq_1 x_\alpha  \leq_1\  1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha},\ \text{and} \\
|x_\alpha&-x_\beta|  \geq \rho c_{\alpha\beta}.
\end{split}
\]
\item If $Q$ is a subdivision of $P$,  the corresponding map $ \mathcal{E}(P)\to \mathcal{E}(Q)$ is given by the map $e_{P,Q}$ defined above. 


\end{enumerate} 
\item We can define an inclusion $\mathcal{E}(P)\to \PK (P)$ by creating the line segment centered at $x_\alpha$ of length $\rho c_{\alpha}$ for $(x_\alpha)_{\alpha\in P}$. These inclusions form a natural transformation $\mathcal{E}\to \PK$. 
\end{enumerate}

\end{defi}
\begin{lem}\label{Lnathomologyiso}
For each $P\in \PP_n$, the inclusion $\mathcal{E}(P)\to \PK(P)$ is a homology isomorphism.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Write $P=\{\alpha_0<\dots \alpha_{p+1}\}$. Let $\Conf_p(D^d)$ be the ordered configuration space of $p$ points in $D^d$. Since the map $\PK(P)\to \Conf_p(D^d)$ which takes a collection of line segments $(l_{\alpha_i})$ to the configuration of centers of $l_{\alpha_i}$ ($i\not=0, p+1$), is a homotopy equivalence, we only have to prove the composition $\mathcal{E}(P)\to \PK(P)\to \Conf_p(D^d)$ is a homology isomorphism. This is codimension $0$ embedding and fits into the following commutative diagram.
\[
\xymatrix{H_*(\mathcal{E}(P))\ar[r]\ar[d] & H_*(\Conf_p(D^d)) \ar[d] \\
H^*(D^{dp}, \Delta'_{\fat}\cup \partial'D^{dp})\ar[r] & H^*(D^{dp}, \Delta_{\fat}\cup \partial D^{dp})}
\]
Here, $D^{dp}=(D^d)^p$, and $\partial' D^{dp}$ is the subspace of elements which do not satisfy at least one of the first two inequalities in (3),(a) of Definition  \ref{Dpunctured} for some $\alpha \in P$ ( so this is a collar of $\partial D^{dp}$). $\Delta'_{\fat}$ is the subspace of elements which do not satisfy the third inequality for some pair $\alpha,\beta \in P$. $\Delta_{\fat}$ is the fat diagonal of $(D^d)^p$.   The vertical arrows are Poincar\'e duality isomorophisms, and the bottom horizontal arrow is induced by the identity. We consider the $\CECHC$ech spectral sequences of pairs $(\Delta'_{\fat}, \Delta'_{\fat}\cap \partial'D^{dp})$ and $(\Delta_{\fat}, \Delta_{\fat}\cap \partial D^{dp})$ with respect to the coverings $\{\Delta'_{\alpha,\beta}\}_{\alpha,\beta}$ and $\{\Delta_{\alpha \beta}\}_{\alpha,\beta}$, where $\Delta'_{\alpha\beta}$ is the subspace of elements which do not satisfy the third inequalities in (3)(a) of Definition  \ref{Dpunctured} for $\alpha,\beta$ and $\Delta_{\alpha\beta}$ is the subspace of elements whose $\alpha$- and $\beta$- components are the same. The inclusion $\Delta_{\alpha\beta}\to \Delta'_{\alpha\beta}$ is clearly a homotopy equivalence. The inclusion $\Delta_{\alpha\beta}\cap \partial D^{dp}\to \Delta'_{\alpha\beta}\cap \partial' D^{dp}$ is also a homotopy equivalence since its homotopy inverse is given by the orthogonal projection to $\Delta_{\alpha\beta}$ followed by the projection to $\partial(D^{dp})$ from the light source $0$. So, the inclusion induces an isomorphism between the relative homology of the pairs. We also see that the inclusion  induces an isomorphism between the relative homology of intersections similarly, so by spectral sequence, we see that the bottom arrow of the square  is an isomorphism.
\end{proof}

\section{Fat diagonal model}\label{SThom}
In this section, we define a functor $\TT:\PP_n^{op}\to \CG_*$ consisting of Thom spaces (or suspensions) of fat diagonals. We prove that this functor is stably equivalent to the Spanier-Whitehead dual of the punctured knot model $\PK$. 

\begin{defi}\label{Dpartition} Let $P\in \PP_n$ be a partition having the minimum piece $\gamma_m$ and the maximum piece $\gamma_M$.
\begin{enumerate}



\item We define a positive number $\eps_P$ by
\[
\eps_P=\frac{\eps}{8^{n-p}},\quad \text{where}\quad p=\# P-2.
\]
Here, $\eps$ is the number fixed in Definition \ref{DPK}.

\item For $P\in \PP_n$ let $\nu_{P}$ be the $\eps_P$-neighborhood of $e_P(\RR^{dp})$ i.e., 
\[
\nu_P=\{y\in \RR^{dn}\mid |y-e_P(x)|<\eps_P \text{ for some } x\in \RR^{dp}\}.
\]  For  $\alpha, \beta\in P-\{\gamma_m,\gamma_M\}$,  we define a   subspace $D_{\alpha\beta}$ of $\RR^{dp}$ by
\[
D_{\alpha\beta}=\{(x_\gamma)_{\gamma \in P-\{\gamma_m, \gamma_M\}} \mid |x_\alpha-x_\beta|\leq d_{\alpha\beta}(P)\}, 
\]
where 
\[
d_{\alpha\beta}(P)=\rho c_{\alpha \beta}-\eps_P.
\]
We denote by $E_{\alpha}$  the subspace of the elements $(x_\gamma)_\gamma$ satisfying the following condition:
\[
\begin{split}
|x_\alpha|& \geq 1-\rho c_\alpha/2+\eps_P,\  \text{or} \\
  x_\alpha&\leq_1\, -1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-\eps_P,\  \text{or} \\  x_\alpha&\geq_1\,  1-\rho c_{\geq \beta}+\eps_P.
\end{split}
\]
Set 
\[
E_P=\bigcup_{\gamma_m<\alpha<\gamma_M}E_\alpha, \qquad D_P=E_P\cup\bigcup_{\gamma_m<\alpha<\beta<\gamma_M}D_{\alpha \beta}.
\] We define a space $\TT(P)\in \CG_*$ by 
\[
\TT(P)=Th(\nu_P)/Th(\nu_P|_{D_P})=\RR^{dn}/\{(\RR^{dn}-\nu_P)\cup \pi_P^{-1}(D_P)\},
\]
where  $\pi_P: \nu_P\to \RR^{pd}$ is the standard projection. If $Q$ is a subdivision of $P$ we define a map $\TT(Q)\to \TT(P)$ as the natural collapsing map and this defines a functor $\TT:(\PP_n)^{op}\to \CG_*$ (see Lemmas  \ref{Ldiagonal_bound} and \ref{Ldiagonal_incl} for well-definedness).\\
\item   Set
\[
\TT_{\emptyset_P}=Th(\nu_P)/Th(\nu_P|_{E_P})=\RR^{dn}/\{(\RR^{dn}-\nu_P)\cup \pi_P^{-1}(E_P)\}.
\]
 For two pieces $\alpha, \beta$ of $P$ with $\gamma_m<\alpha<\beta<\gamma_M$, let $\TT_{\alpha\beta}$ denotes the subspace of $\TT_{\emptyset_P}$ consisting of the basepoint and the elements represented by  $x\in \nu_P$ satisfying $\pi_P(x)\in D_{\alpha\beta}$.

\end{enumerate}
\end{defi}

\begin{lem}\label{Ldiagonal_bound}
Let $Q$ be a subdivision of a partition $P\in \PP_n$. We have
\[
(\RR^{dn}-\nu_Q)\cup (\nu_Q|_{E_Q})\subset (\RR^{dn}-\nu_P)\cup (\nu_P|_{E_P}).
\]
In particular, the identity on $\RR^{dn}$ induces a collapsing map 
\[
\delta'_{P,Q}:\TT_{\emptyset_Q}\to \TT_{\emptyset_P}.
\]
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
 Let $P'\subset P$ (resp. $Q'\subset Q$) denote the subset of pieces which are neither the minimum nor  maximum. Let $y\in \RR^{dn}$.
Write
\[
\begin{split}
\pi'_{Q}(y)&=(x_\gamma)_{\gamma\in Q'}, \\
\pi'_{P}(y)&=(\bar x_{\gamma'})_{\gamma'\in P'},\\
e_{P,Q}(\pi'_P(y))&=(x'_\gamma)_{\gamma\in Q'} .
\end{split}
\] Here, $\pi'_Q:\RR^{dn}\to e_Q(\RR^{dq})=\RR^{dq}$ is the orthogonal projection, where $q=\#Q'$, and  $\pi'_P$ is the similar map. Suppose $y\in \nu_P$.  Since the image of $e_P$ is contained in the image of $e_Q$ and the map $\pi'_{Q}$ sends $y$ to its closest point in the image of $e_Q$, we have
\begin{equation}
|y-e_Q(x_\gamma)|\leq |y-e_Q(x'_\gamma)|=|y-e_P(\pi'_P(y))|< \eps_P\ (<\eps_Q). \label{Eemb}
\end{equation}
So we see $y\in \nu_Q$. This means $\RR^{dn}-\nu_Q\subset \RR^{dn}-\nu_P$. We shall show $\nu_Q|_{E_Q}\subset (\RR^{dn}-\nu_P)\cup \nu_P|_{E_P}$. Let $\alpha\in Q'$ be a piece and $\alpha'$ be the piece of $P$ including $\alpha$. Suppose $|x_\alpha|\geq 1-\rho c_\alpha/2+\eps_Q$. If $y\not\in \nu_P$, we have nothing to prove, so suppose further $y\in \nu_P$. 
By the inequality (\ref{Eemb}), we have
\[
|e_Q(x'_\gamma)-e_Q(x_\gamma)|\leq |e_Q(x'_\gamma)-y|+|y-e_Q(x_\gamma)|\leq 2\eps_P.
\]
As $e_Q$ is a composition of diagonal map with parallel transport, we have
\begin{equation}
|(x'_\gamma)_\gamma-(x_\gamma)_\gamma |\leq 2\eps_P \quad (\text{so} \ |x'_\gamma-x_\gamma|\leq 2\eps_P\ \text{for each}\ \gamma\in Q'). \label{Edifference}
\end{equation}
So we have $|x'_\gamma-x_\gamma|\leq 2\eps_P$ for each $\gamma\in Q'$.  Let $\beta\subset \alpha'$ be the  set of elements smaller than the minimum of $\alpha$. We easily see 
\begin{equation}
\bar x_{\alpha'}-x'_\alpha=_1\frac{\rho}{2}(c_{\alpha'}-2c_\beta-c_\alpha). \label{Earrange}
\end{equation}
Putting these (in)equalities together, we see
\[
\begin{split}
|\bar x_{\alpha'}|=|\bar x_{\alpha'}|& \geq |x_\alpha|-|x_\alpha-x'_\alpha|-|x'_\alpha-\bar x_{\alpha'}| \\
& \geq 1-\rho c_\alpha/2+\eps_Q-2\eps_P-\rho(c_{\alpha'}-c_\alpha)/2 \\
& \geq 1-\rho c_{\alpha'}/2+\eps_P
\end{split}
\]
since we see 
\[
\eps_Q-2\eps_P=\frac{1-2\cdot 8^{p-q}}{8^{n-q}}\geq \frac{1-2/8}{8^{n-q}}>\eps_P.
\]
We have shown that the first inequality in Definition \ref{Dpartition}(2) for $Q$ and the condition $y\in \nu_P$ imply the corresponding inequality for $P$. 
Suppose $x_\alpha\leq_1 -1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-\eps_Q$ and $y\in \nu_P$. Let $\alpha'$ be  as above.
We see 
\[
\begin{split}
\bar x_{\alpha'} & =\bar x_{\alpha'}-x'_\alpha+x'_\alpha-x_\alpha+x_\alpha \\
& \leq_1 \frac{\rho}{2}(c_{\alpha'}-2c_\beta-c_\alpha)+2\eps_P+(-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-\eps_Q \\
&=-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha'}-(\eps_Q-2\eps_P)  \quad (\because \ c_{\leq \alpha}+(c_{\alpha'}-2c_\beta-c_\alpha)/2=c_{\leq \alpha'}) \\
&<-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha'}-\eps_P.
\end{split}
\]
This is  the second inequality in Definition \ref{Dpartition}(2) for $P$. 
Similarly, we see $\bar x_{\alpha'}\geq_1 1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha'}+\eps_P$ if $ x_{\alpha}\geq_1 1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha}+\eps_P$ and $y\in \nu_P$. Thus, we have shown the claimed inclusion.
\end{proof}
\begin{lem}\label{Ldiagonal_incl}
Let $Q$ be a subdivision of $P$. Let $\alpha, \beta \in Q$ be two pieces which are neither minimum nor maximum and satify $\alpha<\beta$.  Let $\alpha', \beta' \in P$ be pieces which include $\alpha$ and $\beta$ respectively. Let $\delta'_{P,Q}:T_{\emptyset_Q}\to T_{\emptyset_P}$ denote the map given in Lemma \ref{Ldiagonal_bound}.
We have 
\[
\delta'_{P,Q}(\TT_{\alpha \beta})\ \subset \
\left\{
\begin{array}{cc}
\{*\} & (\text{if $\alpha'=\beta'$ or $\alpha'$ is the minimum of $P$ or $\beta'$ is the maximum of $P$}), \\
\TT_{\alpha', \beta'} & (\text{ otherwise }).
\end{array}
\right.
\] 

\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Let $y\in \RR^{dn}$ be an element. We use the same notations $P', Q', \pi'_P, \pi'_Q, x_\gamma, x'_\gamma$, and $\bar x_{\gamma'}$ as in the proof of Lemma  \ref{Ldiagonal_bound}.

 We shall show the claim in the case $\alpha'=\beta'$. We assume $\delta'_{P,Q}(y)\not =*$. So $y\in \nu_P$. 
By the inequality (\ref{Edifference}) in the proof of Lemma  \ref{Ldiagonal_bound} and the definition of the map $e_{P,Q}$, we have the following inequality.
\[
\begin{split}
|x_\alpha-x_\beta| & \geq |x'_\alpha-x'_\beta|-|x_\alpha-x'_\alpha|-|x_\beta-x'_\beta| \\
                          & \geq \rho c_{\alpha\beta}-4\eps_P>d_{\alpha\beta}(Q).
\end{split}
\]
This inequality implies $\pi_{Q}(y)\not\in D_{\alpha\beta}$. \\
We shall show the claim in the case that $\alpha'$ is the minimum. It is enough to show the case of $\beta=\beta'$ since general subdivisions factor through the one of this case. In this case, by definition of $e_{P,Q}$, $x'_\alpha=(-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha})u$. Suppose $\delta'_{P,Q}(y)\not=*$ and $\pi_Q(y)\in D_{\alpha\beta}$. We have 
\[
\begin{split}
x_\beta&=  x'_\alpha+(x_\beta-x_\alpha)-(x'_\alpha-x_\alpha) \\
 &\leq _1-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha} +|x_\beta-x_\alpha|+|x'_\alpha-x_\alpha| \\
&\leq -1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}+\rho c_{\alpha\beta}-\eps_Q+2\eps_P \\
&< -1 +\rho c_{\leq \beta}-\eps_P.
\end{split}
\]
So we have the claim. The case that $\beta'$ is the minimum is completely similar.\\
 We shall show the remaining part of the claim. It is enough to the case that either of the minimum or maximum of $\alpha'$ belongs to $\alpha$. 
Clearly, we have $e_{P,Q}(\bar x_{\gamma'})=(x'_\gamma)$. As we see in the case of $\alpha'=\beta'$, we have
\[
\begin{split}
|x'_\alpha-x'_\beta|\leq  &  |x'_\alpha-x_\alpha|+|x_\alpha-x_\beta|+|x_\beta-x'_\beta| \\
\leq & 4\eps_P+d_{\alpha\beta}(Q)
=\rho c_{\alpha\beta}-\eps_Q+4\eps_P.
\end{split}
\]
 By the equality (\ref{Earrange}) in the proof of Lemma  \ref{Ldiagonal_bound}, we have 
\[
|\bar x_{\alpha'}-x'_\alpha|=\rho (c_{\alpha'}-c_{\alpha})/2.
\]
By similar equality for $\beta$, we see
\[
\begin{split}
|\bar x_{\alpha'}-\bar x_{\beta'}|\leq  & |\bar x_{\alpha'}-x'_\alpha|+|x'_\alpha-x'_\beta|+ |\bar x_{\beta'}-x'_\beta| \\
\leq &\rho (c_{\alpha'}-c_{\alpha})/2+(\rho c_{\alpha\beta}-\eps_Q+4\eps_P)+\rho (c_{\beta'}-c_{\beta})/2\\
<&   d_{\alpha',\beta'}(P).
\end{split}
\]
Thus, we have shown the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{defi}\label{Dspectrafunctor}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A {\em spectrum} $X$ is a sequence of pointed spaces $X_0, X_1, \dots$ with structure map $S^1\wedge X_k\to X_{k+1}$ for each $k\geq 0$. A morphism (or map) $f:X\to Y$ of spectra is a sequence of pointed maps $f_0:X_0\to Y_0, f_1:X_1\to Y_1,\dots$ compatible with the structure maps. Let $\SP$ denote the category of spectra and their maps. For a spectrum $X$,  $\pi_k(X)$ denotes the colimit of the sequence $\pi_k(X_0)\to \pi_{k+1}(X_1)\to \cdots $ defined by the structure maps. A map $f:X\to Y$ is called a {\em stable homotopy equivalence} if it induces an isomorphism $\pi_k(X)\to \pi_k(Y)$ for any integer $k$.
\item We define a functor $\TT^S: \PP_n^{op}\to \SP$ as follows. Set $\TT^S(P)_k=S^{k-dn}\wedge \TT(P)$ if $k\geq dn$,  and $\TT^S(P)_k=*$ otherwise. These spaces form a spectra with the obvious structure map. The map corresponding to a map $P\to Q$ is also obviously induced from that of $\TT$. 
\item For a positive number $\delta$, we define a spectrum $\Sphere_\delta$ as follows. We set $\Sphere_{\delta, k}=\{ y\in \RR^k\}/\{y \mid |y|\geq \delta\}$. The structure map $S^1\wedge \Sphere_{\delta, k}\to \Sphere_{\delta, k+1}$ is the obvious collapsing map.
\item We define a functor $\mathcal{E}^\dagger:\PP_n^{op}\to \SP$ as follows.
Set $\mathcal{E}^{\dagger}(P)=\Map(\mathcal{E}(P)_+,\Sphere_{\delta})$ where  $\delta =\eps_P/4$. For a map $P\to Q$, the corresponding map is the restriction of the source followed by the pushforward by the collapsing map $\Sphere_{\eps_Q/4}\to \Sphere_{\eps_P/4}$.

\item We define a functor $\mathcal{E}^\vee:\PP_n^{op}\to \SP$ as follows.
Set $\mathcal{E}^{\vee}(P)=\Map(\mathcal{E}(P)_+,\Sphere)$  For a map $P\to Q$, the corresponding map is the restriction of the source.
\item We define a map $\TPhi= \TPhi_{P,k}:\RR^k\to \EEE^\dagger (P)_k$ by
\[
\RR^k\ni y\longmapsto \{(x_\gamma) \mapsto (y-(0,e_P(x_\gamma))\}\in\EEE^\dagger(P)/ 
\]
$\TT^S_k$ is naturally identified with Thom space associated to the  tubular neighborhood  $\RR^{k-dn}\times \nu_P$ of the embedding $0\times e_P:\RR^{dp}\to \RR^k$ (with some extra collapsed points). $\Phi_{P,k}$ factors through $\TT^S(P)_k$ as in Theorem  \ref{TAtiyahdual}, and  these maps form a natural transformation $\Phi : \TT^S\to \EEE^\dagger$. We see that this is well-defined below.
\item A natural transformation $p_* :\EEE^\vee\to \EEE^\dagger$ is defined by the pushforward by the collapsing map $p :\Sphere\to \Sphere^{\delta}$. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{defi}
The following equivalence is a variation of the one given in \cite{moriya1}. If it is projected to the stable homotopy category, it is a special case of Atiyah duality which is also a special case of $S$-duality. We need point-set level compatibility so we have been taking care about parameters. 
\begin{thm}\label{TAtiyahdual}
Under the notations of Definition \ref{Dspectrafunctor},
the map $\Phi$ is well-defined, and the two maps $\Phi$ and $p_*$ are stable homotopy equivalences.
\end{thm}
\begin{proof}
We shall show the map $\TPhi$ factors through $\TT^S(P)_k$. For notational simplicity, we consider the case of $k=dn$. The other cases will follow completely similarly. It is clear that $\TPhi(\RR^{dn}-\nu_P)=\{*\}$. Let $y\in \RR^k$ be an element with $\TPhi(y)\not= *$.  There exists an element $(x_\gamma)\in \EEE(P)$ such that  $| y- e_P(x_\gamma)|<\eps_P/4$ holds. So we have $|y- e_P(\pi_{P}y)|<\eps_P/4$ and
\[
|\pi_P y-(x_\gamma)|\leq|e_P(\pi_P y)-e_P(x_\gamma)|\leq | e_P(\pi_P y)-y|+|y-e_P(x_\gamma)|<\eps_P/2
\]
 If we write  $\pi_P(y)=(\bar x_\gamma)$, it follows that $|\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha|<\eps_P/2$ for each non-minimum and non-maximum $\alpha\in P$. 
We see
\[
\begin{split}
|\bar x_\alpha| & \leq |x_\alpha|+|\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha| \\
& \leq 1-\rho c_\alpha/2+\eps_P/2<1-\rho c_\alpha/2+\eps_P, \\
\bar x_\alpha  & =x_\alpha+(\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha) \\
&\geq_1-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-|\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha|  \\
& \geq -1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-\eps_P/2>-1+\rho c_{\leq \alpha}-\eps_P, \\
\bar x_\alpha & \leq x_\alpha+(\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha)  \\
 & \leq_1 1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha} +|\bar x_\alpha-x_\alpha|  \\
& \leq 1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha}+\eps_P/2<1-\rho c_{\geq \alpha}+\eps_P. \\
\end{split}
\]
These inequalities imply $\Phi(\pi_P^{-1}(E_\alpha))=*$ in the notations of Definition  \ref{Dpartition}. We also see
\[
\begin{split}
|\bar x_\alpha -\bar x_\beta| & \geq  |x_\alpha-x_\beta|-|x_\alpha-\bar x_\alpha |-|x_\beta-\bar x_\beta| \\
 & > \rho c_{\alpha\beta}-\eps_P  
  = d_{\alpha\beta}(P).
\end{split}
\]
This implies $\TPhi(\pi_P^{-1}(D_{\alpha\beta}))=*$. Thus, $\TPhi$ factors through $\TT^S$. Now the claim of the theorem follows from the classical Atiyah duality (see \cite{browder} for example).





\end{proof}
\begin{rem}\label{RMalin}
In \cite{malin}, Malin  proved homotopy invariance of homogeneous layers of stable embedding calculus tower using  a duality similar to Theorem \ref{TAtiyahdual}.
\end{rem}

\section{Spectral sequences}
\begin{defi}\label{Dgraph}
\begin{enumerate}
\item  For a partition $P\in \PP_n$, a {\em graph $G$ on   $P$} consists of the set of vertexes $V(G)=P$, a finite set of edges $E(G)$ and a map $\phi_G:E(G)\to P_{1,2}(P)$ called the {\em incidence map}, where $P_{1,2}(P)=\{S\subset P\mid \# S=1 \text{ or } 2\}$. So, the vertexes of $G$ are the pieces of $P$. We say that an element of $\phi_G(e)$ is {\em incident with an edge $e$}.  An edge $e$ is called a {\em loop} if $\#\phi_G(e)=1$. two edges $e,e'$ are called {\em double edges} if $\phi_G(e)=\phi_G(e')$ and $\#\phi_G(e)=2$. (Other edges may have the same incident  vertexes  as double edges.) Let $\TGG(P)$ denote the set of all graphs on  $P$. Let $\gamma_m$ and $\gamma_M$ be the minimum and maximum pieces of $P$, respectively. 
$\GG(P)\subset \TGG(P)$ denotes the subset of graphs with the edge set $E(G)\subset \{(\alpha,\beta)\mid \alpha, \beta \in P,\ \gamma_m<\alpha<\beta<\gamma_M\}$ and the natural incidence map $(\alpha,\beta)\mapsto \{\alpha,\beta\}$. The set $E(G)$ of a graph $G\in \GG(P)$ is regarded as a totally ordered set by the lexicographical order. Let $\emptyset_P\in \GG(P)$ denote the graph with  empty edge set. We  sometimes denote a graph in $\GG(P)$ by a formal product of edges (see Example \ref{Egraph} below).   Let $e$ be the $i$-th edge of $G\in \GG(P)$. $\partial_eG$ (or $\partial_iG$) denotes the subgraph of $G$ with $E(\partial_eG)=E(G)-\{e\}$. Similarly, if $e'$ is the $j$-th edge, $\partial_{ee'}G$ (or $\partial_{ij}G$) denotes the subgraph made by removing $e$ and $e'$. For two vertexes $\alpha, \beta$ of a graph $G$, we write $\alpha\sim_G\beta$ when $\alpha$ and $\beta$ belong to the same connected component of $G$. By abusing notations, for $i\in [n+1]$, we write $i\sim_G\beta$ if there is a (unique) piece $\alpha$ satisfying $i\in \alpha$ and $\alpha\sim_G\beta$. Similarly, if $i$ belongs to a piece $\alpha$ which belongs to a connected component $S$ of $G$, we write $i\in S$ instead of $i\in \alpha\in S$.   


\item For a map $P\to Q$ of partitions,  $\delta_{P,Q}:Q\to P$ denotes the map of sets sending  $\alpha\in Q$ to the piece of $P$ containing $\alpha$. This map induces a map $\delta_{P,Q}:\TGG(Q)\to \TGG(P)$. The  graph $\delta_{P,Q}(G)$ has the same edge set as $G$  and the incidence map  given by the composition
\[
E(G)\stackrel{\phi_G}{\to}P_{1,2}(P)\stackrel{(\delta_{P,Q})_*}{\to}P_{1,2}(Q).
\] For a graph $G\in \GG(Q)$, if $\delta_{P,Q}(G)$ has no loop nor double edges and the minimum and maximum pieces of $P$ are not incident with any edge of $\delta_{P,Q}(G)$, we always identify $\delta_{P,Q}(G)$ with a unique graph in $\GG(P)$ which has the same image of the incidence map. If $P$ is made by unifying the $i+1$-th and $i+2$-th pieces of $Q$, we denote $P$ and $\delta_{P,Q}$ by $\delta_iQ$ and $\delta_i$, respectively. Similarly, if $P$ is made by unifying each of  the $i+1$-th and $i+2$-th pieces, and  $j+1$-th and $j+2$-th pieces, $P$ and $\delta_{P,Q}$ are  denoted by $\delta_{ij}Q$ and  $\delta_{ij}$. $\delta_{ijk}$ is similarly understood.

\end{enumerate}
\end{defi}
\begin{exa}\label{Egraph}
Let $G_1, G_2 \in \GG([5])$ be the graphs  with two edges given by
\[
G_1=(1,4)(2,3),\quad G_2=(1,3)(2,4).
\]
These graphs are drawn  in Figure \ref{Fgraphs_ch2}. The straight line is not a part of data, and  the chords denote the edges. The vertexes are intersections of the line and chords. They are labeled  by $\{1\},\dots, \{4\}$ in the order from left to right. The minimum  and maximum vertexes $\{0\}, \{5\}$ are usualy omitted.  We have $E(\partial_1G_1)=\{(2,3)\}$ and $E(\partial_2G_1)=\{(1,4)\}$. $G_1$ has the four connected components $\{\{0\}\}$, $\{\{1\},\{4\}\}$, $\{\{2\},\{3\}\}$, and $\{\{5\}\}$. By definition, we have
\[
\delta_1[5]=\{\{0\},\{12\},\{3\},\{4\},\{5\}\}, 
\]
and $\delta_1G_1=\delta_1G_2$ since $E(\delta_1G_1)=E(\delta_1G_2)=\{(\{12\},\{3\}),(\{12\},\{4\})\}$. Similarly, we have $\delta_3G_1=\delta_3G_2$. The graph $\delta_1G_1$ has the three connected components $\{\{0\}\}$, $\{\{12\},\{3\}, \{4\}\}$, and $\{\{5\}\}$. 
\end{exa}
\begin{defi}\label{Dtriple}
 For a graph $G\in \GG(P)$, set
$\TT_G=\bigcap_{(\alpha,\beta)\in E(G)} \TT_{\alpha\beta}\subset T_{\emptyset_P}$. \\
\indent We define a triple complex $\TTH_{\bullet * \star}$ as follows. 
As a module, we set 
\[
\TTH_{p q s}=\bigoplus_{P, G}\bar C_q(\TT_G)
\]
where $P$ runs through partitions of $P_{n}$ such that $\# P=p+2$ and $G$ runs through
graphs of $\GG(P)$ such that $\#E(G)=s$. $\TTH$ has three differentials $\delta, d, \partial$ of degree $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, $(0,0,1)$. $d$ denotes the differential on singular chains, $\partial$ is the Cech differential given by
\[
\partial=\sum_{1\leq k\leq s}(-1)^{k-1}\partial_k,
\]
where $\partial_k:\bar C_q(\TT_G)\to \bar C_q(\TT_{\partial_kG})$ is the pushforward by the inclusion. To define $\delta$, we shall define a map $\delta_i$ of degree $(1,0,0)$. 
Put $P=\{\alpha_0<\cdots \alpha_{p+1}\}$.  If $\delta_iG$  has a loop or double edge, or at least one of the connected components of $\delta_iG$ which include the minimum or maximum is not discrete, we set $\delta_i=0$ on $\bar C_*(\TT_G)$. Suppose otherwise  i.e., $\delta_iG$ belongs to $\GG(P_i)$.
 The set of edges of $G$ whose smaller incident vertex  is $\alpha_i$ or $\alpha_{i+1}$ and the set of edges of $\delta_i(G)$ whose smaller incident vertex is $\alpha_i\cup \alpha_{i+1}$ are one-to-one correspondence via the incidence map. This correspondence induces the permutation $\sigma_{G,i}$ of the lexicographical order of the edges. We set $\delta_i=sgn(\sigma_{G,i})(\delta'_{i})_*$. Here, $\delta'_i:\TT_G \to \TT_{\delta_iG}$ is the collapsing map for subdivision $P$ of $\delta_iP$ given in Lemma \ref{Ldiagonal_incl}. (By definition of $\TT_G$ and the same lemma, the image of $\delta'_i$ is contained in $\TT_{\delta_iG}$.) We set 
$\delta=\sum_{i=0}^{p}(-1)^i\delta_i $.

\end{defi}
\begin{lem}\label{Lwelldeftriple}
The triple complex $\TTH$ is well defined. i.e., the three differentials are commutative without signs.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Only non-trivial commutativity is that of $\partial$ with $\delta$. Let $e$ be the $k$-th edge among those whose smaller incident vertex is $\alpha_i$. Suppose exactly $m$ edges pass through $e$ by $\sigma_{G,i}$. On $G$, the sign in removing $e$ is $(-1)^k$ and On $\delta_iG$, the sign in removing the corresponding edge is $(-1)^{k+m}$. On the other hand, we have $sgn(\sigma_{G,i})=sgn(\sigma_{\partial_eG, i})$. So we have the commutativity.
\end{proof}

\section{Condensed maps}\label{Scondensed}
In this section, we define a class of maps used to define chains in the triple complexes $\TTH$ and prove their properties. 
\begin{defi}\label{Dcondensed}

\begin{enumerate}
%\item Let $P\in \PP_n$ be a partition. 
%A graph is  a star if it has exactly $k$ vertexes and $k-1$ edges and  valence of one of its vertexes is  $k-1$ and the others are $1$ for some $k\geq 1$, as usual. A graph $G\in \GG(P)$ is a componentwise star if each  connected full subgraph is a star.
\item Let $X$ be an unpointed topological space and $G\in \GG(P)$  a graph.  A map $f=(f_1,\dots, f_n):X\to \RR^{dn}$ is {\em $G$-condensed} if the following conditions hold.
\begin{enumerate}
\item $f$ is proper. %i.e., preimage of compact set is compact.
\item For each connected component $S$ of $G$,  there is a vertex $\alpha\in S$, called {\em a base of $S$ (for $(G,f)$)}, which satisfies  the following conditions.
\begin{enumerate}
%\item $\alpha$ is of valence $\geq 2$ if $\# S\geq 3$, 
\item For each $\beta\in S$ and each $i\in\beta$,  $f_i(x)$ belongs to the convex hull of $\{f_j(x)\mid j\in \alpha\}$,
\item there is no edge in $S$ both of whose incident vertexes are smaller than $\alpha$.
\end{enumerate}
(A discrete vertex is regarded as a base.)
\end{enumerate}
\item An edge $e$ of a graph $G$ is called a {\em bridge} if $\#\pi_0(\partial_eG)>\#\pi_0(G)$.
\item Let $f:X\to \RR^{dn}$ be a  map, and $e=(\alpha,\beta),\ e'$  two bridges of a graph $G$. For $1\leq i\leq n$ and $s\in [0,\infty)$, we set 
\[
A_{e}^i(s)=\left\{\begin{array}{cl}
s & \text{if $i\sim _{\partial_eG} \alpha$} \\
-s & \text{if $i\sim _{\partial_eG} \beta$} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{array}
\right.
\]
and define $A_{e}:[0,\infty)\to \RR^{dn}$
\[
A_{e}(s)=(A_{e}^i(s)v)_{1\leq i\leq n},
\]
where $v=(0,1,0,\dots, 0)$ as in subsection \ref{SSNT}. The {\em contracting homotopy $F:X\times [0,\infty)\to \RR^{dn}$ of $f$  in removing $e$ from $G$} (in short, {\em $e$-contraction of $f$ for $G$}) is   defined by
\[
F(x,s)=f(x)+A_e(s).
\]
The {\em $(e,e')$-contraction  $F':X\times [0,\infty)^2\to \RR^{dn}$ of $f$ for $G$}  is defined by
\[
F'(x,s_1,s_2)=f(x)+A_e(s_1)+A_{e'}(s_2).
\]

\item For two  maps $f,g:X\to \RR^{dn}$, the 
{\em straight homotopy $h:X\times I\to \RR^{dn}$ from $f$ to $g$} is defined by
$h(x,t)=(1-t)f(x)+tg(x)$.
\item Let $G\in \GG([n+1])$ be a graph with exactly $4$ connected components. Define a map  $f_G=(f_1,\dots f_n):\RR^{2d}\to \RR^{dn}$ by
\[
f_i(x,y)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc}
x & (i\sim_G1)\\
y & (\text{otherwise}).
\end{array}\right.
\]
Clearly, $f_G$ is $G$-condensed, and a vertex is a base if it satisfies the condition (ii) on a base. So, the minimum and the second to minimum vertexes of each connected component are  bases.
\item For a graph $G\in \GG(P)$, we define subsets $U_G$ and $U^1_G$ of $\RR^{dn}$ by
\[
\begin{split}
U_G=&(\RR^{dn}-\nu_{P})\cup \pi_P^{-1}(\cap_{(\alpha,\beta)\in E(G)}D_{\alpha\beta}), \\
U_G^1=&(\RR^{dn}-p_1^{-1}p_1(\nu_P))\cup p_1^{-1} p_1(\cap_{(\alpha,\beta)\in E(G)}\pi_P^{-1}D_{\alpha\beta}).
\end{split}
\]
Here, $p_1:\RR^{dn}\to \RR^n$ is  the product of the projection to the first coordinate $\RR^d\to \RR$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{defi}

Set $n=4$ and let $G_1=(1,4)(2,3), G_2=(1,3)(2,4)\in \GG([5])$.
 $f_{G_1}$ (resp. $f_{G_2}$) is given by $(x,y)\mapsto (x,y,y,x)$ (resp. $(x,y)\mapsto (x,y,x,y)$). Put $e=(1,4)$ and $e'=(2,3)$. The $e$-contraction of $f_{G_1}$ for $G_1$ is given by $(x,y,s)\mapsto (x+sv,y,y, x-sv)$ and the $(e,e')$-contraction of $f_{G_1}$ for $G_1$ is given by $(x,y,s_1,s_2)\mapsto (x+s_1v, y+s_2v, y-s_2v, x-s_1v)$. $f_{G_1}$ is also $\delta_1G_1$-condensed. $\{1,2\}$ is a base of a connected component of $\delta_1G_1$. The other vertexes (except for $\{0\}$ and $\{5\}$) are not bases. The $e$-contraction of $f_{G_1}$ for $\delta_1G_1$ is given by $(x,y,s)\mapsto (x+sv, y+sv, y+sv, x-sv)$ and is different from that for $G_1$ (since connected components are different). The straight homotopy $h$ from $f_{G_1}$ to $f_{G_2}$ is given by $(x,y,t)\mapsto (x,y,(1-t)y+tx, (1-t)x+ty)$. This is also $\delta_1G_1$-condensed. its base is $\{1,2\}$.





\end{exa}

\begin{lem}\label{Lcondensed}
Let $f:X\to \RR^{dn}$ be a $G$-condensed map. We have $f(X) \subset U_G\cap U^1_G$. In particular, $f$ induces a map $f:X^*\to \TT_G$.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Let $S$ be a connected component of $G$,  $\alpha=\{j,j+1,\dots,k\}$ and  $\beta=\{l,\dots, m\}$  two vertexes of $S$.  For $x\in X$, suppose $f(x)\in \nu_P$. Write $\pi_P(f(x))=(y_\gamma)$. First suppose further that $\alpha$ is a base of $S$.  By definitions of $e_P$ and $\nu_P$, for $i\in \beta$, we have
\[
|f_i(x)-y_\beta|\leq \rho \frac{c_\beta-\min\{c_{l}, c_{m}\}}{2}+\eps_P.
\]
Since $f_i(x)$ belongs to the convex hull of $\{f_r(x)\}_{r\in \alpha}$, we have 
\[
|f_i(x)-y_\alpha|\leq \rho \frac{c_\alpha-\min\{c_{j}, c_{k}\}}{2}+\eps_P.
\]
So, we have $|y_\alpha-y_\beta|<\rho (c_\alpha+c_\beta)/2-\eps_P\leq d_{\alpha\beta}(P)$, which implies $\pi_P(f(x))\in D_{\alpha\beta}$. If neither of $\alpha$ nor $\beta$ is a base, we take a base $\gamma$ of $S$. By definition of the base, we may assume $\alpha<\gamma<\beta$ or $\gamma<\alpha<\beta$. By the above argument, 
we see 
\[
\begin{split}
|y_\alpha-y_\beta|&<\frac{\rho}{2}(c_\alpha+c_\beta+2c_\gamma-\min\{c_{l}, c_{m}\}-\min\{c_{j}, c_{k}\})+4\eps_P\\
&<d_{\alpha\beta}(P).
\end{split}
\] The second inequality follows from  the condition on $c_r$ imposed in Definition \ref{DPK} (1). We have shown $f(X)\subset U_G$. The proof of the inclusion to $U^1_G$ is completely similar. Since $f$ is proper, $f$ induces the map from the one-point compactification.
\end{proof}
\begin{lem}\label{Lcontraction}
Let $Q$ be a partition, $G\in \GG(Q)$ a graph, and  $f:X\to \RR^{dn}$  a $G$-condensed map.
\begin{enumerate}
\item  Let $e$ be a bridge of $G$ such that each non-discrete connected component of $\partial_eG$ contains a base of some connected component of $G$. The $e$-contraction $F$ of $f$ is $\partial_eG$-condensed. 
\item  Let $e, e'$ be two  bridges of $G$ such that each non-discrete connected component of $\partial_{ee'}G$ contains a base of some connected component of $G$. The $(e,e')$-contraction $F$ of $f$ is $\partial_{ee'}G$-condensed.
\item  Set $P=\delta_iQ$. Suppose that
 $\delta_iG\in \GG(P)$ and
 both of the $i+1$-th and $i+2$-th pieces of $Q$ are bases for $f$. Then, $f$ is $\delta_iG$-condensed.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
For part 1, we only have to prove that $F$ is proper. The other conditions are clear by definition. Let $\beta$ and $\gamma$ be the vertexes incident with $e$, $\alpha$  a base of the connected component of $G$ including $e$.  Say $\alpha\not\sim_{\partial_eG}\beta$ and $\gamma<\beta$. Suppose $F(x)\in D^{dn}_R=(D^d_R)^n$, the product of disks of radius $R$. For $i\in \beta$ and $j\in \alpha$, we have
\[
2R\geq |F_i(x)-F_j(x)|=|f_i(x)- sv-(f_j(x)+ sv)|\geq ||f_i(x)-f_j(x)|-2s|.
\]  
This implies $s\leq R+|f_i(x)-f_j(x)|/2$. Since the convex hulls of  $\{f_j(x)+sv\}_{j\in \alpha}$ and $\{f_j(x)\}_{j\in \alpha}$ are congruent and the diameter of former one is  $2R$ or less. So, we have $|f_i(x)-f_j(x)|\leq 2R$. By these inequalities,  we  have $s\leq 2R$, which implies $|f_k(x)|\leq 3R$ for a number $k$ belonging to a piece included in $S$. Thus, we have 
$F^{-1}(D^{dn}_R)\subset f^{-1}(D^{dn}_{3R})$ and conclude that $F$ is proper.  Other conditions of $\partial_eG$-condensed map is clear from the assumption. Parts 2 and  3 are similar. 
\end{proof}

\begin{lem}\label{Lcondensed_collapse}

 Let $P\to Q\in \PP_n$ be a subdivision, $G\in\GG(Q)$ a graph  with an edge $e=(\alpha,\beta)$.

\begin{enumerate}
\item 

Let  $f:X\to \RR^{dn}$ be a proper map whose image is contained in $U^1_G$.  Suppose that either of the following two conditions holds.
\begin{enumerate}
 \item $\alpha\cup \beta$ is included in a  piece of $P$.
\item At least one of $\alpha$, $\beta$ is included in either of the minimum or maximum of $P$.
\end{enumerate} Then, the induced map $f :X^*\to \TT_{\emptyset_P}$ is $*$ (the constant map to the base point). In particular, if $f$ is the $e$-contraction of some $G$-condensed map for $G$, or more generally, $f_l=_1g_l$  ($1\leq l\leq n$)  for some $G$-condensed map $g$, the induce map to $\TT_{\emptyset_P}$ is $*$.
\item Let $f:X\to \RR^{dn}$ be a $G$-condensed map. Let $\gamma$ be a piece of  $Q$ with $\beta>\alpha<\gamma$, Suppose that $\alpha$ is a base for $f$, $\gamma\sim _G\alpha $,  and $\beta\cup\gamma$ is included in a piece of $P$. The $e$-contraction of $f$ followed by the collapsing map $\delta_{P,Q}|_{\TT_{\partial_eG}}:\TT_{\partial_eG}\to \TT_{\emptyset_P}$ is the constant map to $*$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
We shall show part 1 for the condition (a). For simplicity, we assume $\alpha<\beta$ and $\alpha$ is a base of $f$. Let $i$ (resp. $j$) be the minimum of $\beta$ (resp. the maximum of $\alpha$.  Suppose $f(x)\in \nu_P$ for some $x\in X$. By definitions of $e_P$ and $\nu_P$, we have
$f_{i}(x)-f_{j}(x)>_1 \rho(c_i+c_j)/2-2\eps_P$. Since $f(x)\in U^1_G\cap \nu_P$, by an argment similar to the proof of Lemma \ref{Ldiagonal_bound}, we have
\[
f_i(x)-f_j(x)<_1\rho(c_i+c_j)/2-\eps_Q+2\eps_P< \rho(c_i+c_j)/2-2\eps_P.
\]
 These inequalities contradict and we have proved part 1. The proofs of other parts are similar.
\end{proof}
\begin{defi}\label{Di-cont}
Let $F=(F_1,\dots, F_n):X\to\RR^{dn}$ be a map.
For $\eps=+$ or $-$, we define a map $F^{i\eps}:X\times [0,\infty)\to \RR^{dn}$ called the {\em $(i,\eps)$-contraction of $f$} as follows.

F^{i\eps}_k(x)=
\left\{

\end{defi}

\begin{lem}\label{Li-cont}
Let $f=(f_1,\dots, f_n):X\to \RR^{dn}$ be a $G$-condensed map for a graph $G\in\GG(P)$, $e, e'$  bridges of $G$ and $i$ a number with $1\leq i\leq n-1$. Say $i$ belongs to the $k+1$-th piece of $P$. Suppose that $i\sim_Gi+1$ and $\delta_k\partial_eG$ (resp. $\delta_k\partial_{ee'}G$) belongs to $\GG(\delta_kP)$.  If any base of the component including $i$ includes $i'=i$ or $i+1$, suppose further that for any $x\in X$ there exist $l,m\not=i,i+1$ such that
\[
|f_{i',1}(x)-f_{l,1}(x)|\leq |f_{m,1}(x)-f_{l,1}(x)|,
\]  
where the subscript $1$ denotes the first coordinate. 
Let $F$ be the $e$-contraction (resp. $(e,e')$-contraction) of $f$ for $G$.
 The $(i,\eps)$-contraction $F^{i\eps}$ of $F$ is proper and its image is contained in  $U_{\delta_k\partial_eG}\cap U^1_{\delta_k\partial_eG}$ (resp. $U_{\delta_k\partial_{ee'}G}\cap U^1_{\delta_k\partial_{ee'}G}$). In particular, $F^{i\eps}$ induces a map $F^{i\eps}:X^*\wedge[0,\infty]\to \TT_{\delta_k\partial_eG}$ (resp. $F^{i\eps}:X^*\wedge[0,\infty]^{\wedge 2}\to \TT_{\delta_k\partial_{ee'}G}$).


\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Put $F=(F_1,\dots F_n)$ and $F^{i\eps}=F'=(F'_1,\dots, F'_n)$. Similarly to the proof of Lemma \ref{Lcondensed}, we can see that $F'$ is proper. (The extra assumption for the case that any base includes one of $i, i+1$ is used here). We shall show $F'(X\times [0,\infty)^2)\subset U_{\delta_kG}$ in the case that $F$ is the $e$-contraction and there is a base $\beta$ which does not include $i,i+1$. Suppose $F'(\tilde x)\in\nu_P$ for $\tilde x=(x,s_1,s_2)\in X\times [0,\infty)^2$. Put $\pi_P(F'(\tilde x))=(y_\gamma)_\gamma$. Let $\alpha\in \delta_kP$ be the piece including $i,i+1$ By definition, we have
\[
|y_\alpha-\frac{1}{2}(F'_i(x)+F'_{i+1}(x))|<\frac{\rho c_\alpha}{2}-50\eps.
\]
Clearly, $\frac{1}{2} (F'_i(x)+F'_{i+1}(x))=\frac{1}{2} (F_i(x)+F_{i+1}(x))$ and the first coordinate of $\frac{1}{2} (F_i(x)+F_{i+1}(x))$ is in the image of projection of the convex hull of $\{F_j(x)\}_{j\in \beta}$ to the first coordinate,  so 
\[
|y_{\beta 1}-\frac{1}{2}(F_{i1}(x)+F_{i+1,1}(x))|<\frac{\rho c_\beta}{2}-50\eps.
\]
By these (in)equalities, we see $|y_{\alpha1}-y_{\beta 1}|<\rho(c_\alpha+c_\beta)/2-100\eps$.  Since  $\alpha\simeq_G\beta$, there exists $i_1\in \alpha$ such that $F_{i_1 }(x)$ is in the convex hull of $\{F_i(x)\}_{l\in \beta}$.  We also have $F'_{i_2,2}(x)=F_{i_2, 2}(x)$ for any $i_2$ by definition, where the extra subscript means the $n-1$-tuple of  the second to $n$-th coordinate. So we see $|y_{\alpha 2}-y_{\beta 2}|<4\eps_P$. Thus, we have shown $|y_\alpha-y_\beta|<d_{\alpha\beta}(P)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Suppose that either of the following conditions holds.
\begin{enumerate}
\item $f_{i}=f_{j}$ and $f_{i+1}=f_{j+1}$, or 
\item $f_{i}=f_{j+1}$ and $f_{i+1}=f_{j} $

The straight homotopy $h$ from  $F^{i\eps}$ to $F^{j\eps'}$  is proper and its image is contained in  $U_{\delta_{kl}\partial_eG}\cap U^1_{\delta_{kl}\partial_eG}$. \item Suppose that $i,i+1,j,j+1$ is contained in a single connected component of $G$ which has a base which does not contain any of the four numbers. 
For any pair $\eps, \eps'=\pm$, the straight homotopy $h$ from  $F^{i\eps}$ to $F^{j\eps'}$  is proper and its image is contained in $U_{\delta_{kl}\partial_eG}\cap U^1_{\delta_{kl}\partial_eG}$ . So, $h$ induces a map $h:X^*\wedge[0,\infty]^{\wedge 2}\wedge(I_+) \to \TT_{\delta_{kl}\partial_eG}$.
\end{enumerate}
A similar claim holds for $(e,e')$-contraction.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
The proof is similar to that of Lemma \ref{Li-cont} so we omit details. The choice of $\eps'$ in part 1 ensures $h$ is proper.
\end{proof}

\begin{lem}\label{Lfundcyc} Let $C$ be the chain defined in Lemma \ref{Lh_i_welldef}, and   $\psi$, $\psi_j$, $\lambda_j$, and $\lambda'_j$ the maps given in Definition \ref{Dchainhomotopy} for $(G,H,i)=(G_1, G_2,3)$.  Put $G_0=\delta_{13}\partial_1G_1$. $G_0$ is the graph with only one edge $(\{12\},\{34\})$. 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Each pushforward which appears as  the terms of $c(G,H,i)$ are annihilated by $\delta_k$ unless $(G,H,i,k)=(G_1,G_2,3,1)$. 
The terms of $c(G_1,G_2,3)$ except for $\psi_j(w_{11})$ is annihilated by $\delta_1$.

\item  
$\delta C=\delta_1(\psi_1(w_{11})+\psi_2(w_{21}))$ is a fundamental cycle of $\TT_{G_0}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
For part 1, we consider the case of $(G,H,i)=(G_1,G_2,1)$. We have $\delta_1\psi(w_{01})=0$ as $\delta_1\partial_1G$ has a loop. The graph  of $\delta'_1\circ\lambda_2$ is $\delta_1\partial_{2}G$. While this does not have a loop nor double edges, by Lemma \ref{Lcondensed_collapse} (1), we have $\delta'_1\circ\lambda_{2}=*$. We also have $\delta'_2\circ\psi_j=*$ by (2) of the same lemma. The other terms vanish similarly.\\
\indent We shall show part 2. 
By definition, $\TT_{G_0}$ is a Thom space associated to the disk bundle $\nu_P|_N$, where $N$ is a open tubular neighborhood of the diagonal $\Delta$ in the product of  disks $(D^2)^2$ (with different radius). Precisely speaking, in the notation of Definition \ref{Dpartition}, $(D^2)^2=\RR^4-E_P$, and $N=D_{\alpha\beta}\cap (\RR^4-E_P)$, where $\alpha=\{12\}, \beta=\{34\}$ and $P=\{\{0\},\alpha, \beta,\{5\}\}$.  So, $\TT_{G_0}$ is homeomorphic to $S^4\wedge S^2\wedge (Int D^2_+)$, where  $S^4$ and $S^2\times\{0\}\subset S^2\wedge (Int D^2_+)$ correspond to a fiber and $\Delta$,  respectively. We consider a retract to $S^4\wedge (S^2\times \{0\})\cong Th(\nu_P|_\Delta)$ and show that the composition of $\psi_j$ with this retract is of degree one. Write $\nu=\nu_P|_N$. The orthogonal projection $r: N\to \Delta$ induces the bundle map $\tilde r: \nu\to \nu|_{\Delta}$ which is given by the parallel transport taking the  center to the center on each fiber. This map induces a map $\tilde r:\TT_{G_0}\to Th(\nu|_{\Delta})$.  We consider $\nu$ and $\nu|_\Delta$ as  subspaces of $\RR^8$. Put 
\[
F_j:=\tilde r\circ \psi_j|_{(\psi_j)^{-1}(\nu)}:(\psi^1_i)^{-1}(\nu)\to \nu|_{\Delta}\qquad \text{for}\qquad j=1,2.
\]
We shall show $F_j$ is a monomorphism and $Im(F_1)\cup Im(F_2)=\nu|_{\Delta}$ and 
$Im(F_1)\cap Im(F_2)=Im(F_1)|_{s=0}=Im(F_2)|_{s=0}$, where $s\in [0,\infty)$ is the variable in the  $e_j$-contraction. These claims imply the lemma. By definition, $\nu$ is the tubular neighborhood of the map
\[
e_P:(a,b)\mapsto \left(a-\frac{\rho}{2}c_2u,\ a+\frac{\rho}{2}c_1u,\ b-\frac{\rho}{2}c_4u,\ b+\frac{\rho}{2}c_3u\right).
\]
The projection $\pi_P$ of $\nu$ sends $(c,d,e,f)\in \RR^8$ to the point $(a,b)$ which minimize the distance $|(c,d,e,f)-e_P(a,b)|$. By elementary calculation, the point is given by
\[
(a,b)=\left(\frac{c+d}{2}+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_2-c_1)u,\ \frac{e+f}{2}+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_4-c_3)u\right).
\]
Similarly, we see that $r:N\to \Delta$ is given by $r(a,b)=(a+b)/2$.
We shall verify $F_1$ is a monomorphism. Since $\psi_1(x,y,s,t)=((1-t)x+ty+sv, tx+(1-t)y-sv, x-sv, y-sv)$, we have 
\[
r\circ \pi\circ \psi_1(x,y,s,t)=\frac{x+y}{2}-\frac{sv}{2}+\frac{\rho}{8}(c_2+c_4-c_1-c_3)u.
\] 
We denote the right hand side by $w$. For simplicity, we move the fiber of $\nu$ over $\pi_P(\psi_1(x,y,s,t))$ by the parallel transport which sends its center to $0$. By this move, $\psi_1(x,y,s,t)$ is sent to 
\[
\psi_1(x,y,s,t)-e_P(\pi_P(\psi(x,y,s,t))=(p_1,-p_1,q, -q)
\] 
where 
\[
p_1=(t-1/2)(y-x)+sv+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_1+c_2)u,\ q=\frac{-1}{2}(y-x)+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_3+c_4)u.
\] Similarly, If $F_2(x,y,s,t)$ is in the fiber over a point sent to $w$ by $r$, the point is given by $(p_2,-p_2, q, -q)$ where 
\[
p_2=(t-1/2)(y-x)-sv+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_1+c_2)u
\] The fiber over $\pi_P$ is a disk of radius $\epsilon_P$. It is enough to show there exists a unique $(x,y,s,t)$ such that $q'=q$ and ($p'=p_1$ or $p'=p_2$) for given point $(p',q',w)$ with $|(p',q')|\leq \epsilon_P$, and for the point $(x,y,s,t)$, $p'=p_1=p_2$ holds if and only if $s=0$. Suppose that $(p', q')=(p_j,q)$. By the above formulas for $p_j$ and $q$, for both of $j=1,2$ we have
\[
t=\frac{{p'}^1-{q'}^1+\rho(c_3+c_4-c_1-c_2)/4}{-2{q'}^1+\rho(c_3+c_4)/2},
\]
where ${p'}^1$ and ${q'}^1$ denote the first components o $p'$ and $q'$, respectively. The assumptions on $c_j$ and $|(p',q')|$ ensure $0<t<1$. Similar computation shows that $s>0$ holds for exactly one of $p_1$, $p_2$ or otherwise, $s=0$ holds for both of $p_1$ and $p_2$.
Thus we have proved the claim.
\end{proof}
\begin{thm}\label{Tdifferential} In dimension $d=2$ and over a field of characteristic $2$, there exists an element  $g\in \EE^{-2,2}_2$ satisfying $d_2(g)\not=0$. 
\end{thm}
\begin{proof}
By degree reason, we may consider $\bar \EE_r$ instead of $\EE_r$. By Lemma \ref{Lspeqseq}, we see the class $g'$ represented by $\sum_{1\leq i\leq 3}c(G_i)$ is lifted to a class $g\in \bar \EE^{-2,2}_1$. By Lemma \ref{Lh_i_welldef}, $d_1g'=0$. Again by Lemma \ref{Lspeqseq}, $d_1g=0$. By Lemma \ref{Lh_i_welldef} and  \ref{Lfundcyc}, we have $d_2g'\not=0$, and we have $d_2g\not=0$.
\end{proof}

By definition, $\TT_{G_0}$ is a Thom space associated to the disk bundle $\nu_P|_N$, where $N$ is a open tubular neighborhood of the diagonal $\Delta$ in the product of  disks $(D^2)^2$ (with different radius). Precisely speaking, in the notation of Definition  \ref{Dpartition}, $(D^2)^2=\RR^4-E_P$, and $N=D_{\alpha\beta}\cap (\RR^4-E_P)$, where $\alpha=\{1,2,3\}, \beta=\{4,5\}$ and $P=\{\{0\},\alpha,\beta,\{6\}\}$. Write $\nu=\nu_P$. Fiber dimension of $\nu$ is $6$. The orthogonal projection $r: N\to \Delta$ induces the bundle map $\tilde r: \nu\to \nu|_{\Delta}$ which is given by the parallel transport taking center to center on each fiber. This map induces a map $\tilde r:\TT_{G_0}\to Th(\nu|_{\Delta})$. $Th(\nu|_{\Delta})$ is homeomorphic to $S^{8}$. We consider $\nu$ and $(\nu|_\Delta)$ as  subspaces of $\RR^{10}$. Put 
\[
F_{kl}:=\tilde r\circ \mu^{+}_{2kl}|_{(\mu^{+}_{2kl})^{-1}(\nu)}:(\mu^+_{2kl})^{-1}(\nu)\to \nu|_{\Delta}\qquad \text{for}\qquad 1\leq k<l\leq 3
\]
We shall show $F_{kl}$ is a monomorphism and $\cup_{k<l}Im(F_{kl})=\nu|_{\Delta}$ and 
$Im(F_{kl})\cap Im(F_{k',l'})$ is contained in the union of the image by $F_{kl}$ of subspace where at least one of the contracting parameters $s_1,s_2$ in the direction of $v$ is zero if $(k,l)\not=(k',l')$. These claims imply the lemma. By definition, $\nu$ is the tubular neighborhood of the map
\[
e_P:(a,b)\mapsto \left(a-\frac{\rho}{2}(c_2+c_3)u,\ a+\frac{\rho}{2}(c_1-c_3)u,\ a+\frac{\rho}{2}(c_1+c_2)u,\ b-\frac{\rho}{2}c_5u,\ b+\frac{\rho}{2}c_4u\right).
\]
The projection $\pi_P$ of $\nu_P$ sends $(c,d,e,f,g)\in \RR^{10}$ to the point $(a,b)$ which minimize the distance $|(c,d,e,f,g)-e_P(a,b)|$. By elementary calculation, the point is given by
\[
(a,b)=\left(\frac{1}{3}(c+d+e+\rho(c_3-c_1)u),\ \frac{1}{2}(f+g+\frac{\rho}{2}(c_4-c_3)u)\right).
\]
Similarly, we see that $r:N\to \Delta$ is given by $r(a,b)=(a+b)/2$.
We shall verify that $F_{kl}$ is a monomorphism. For a while, we omit the subscript $kl$  and $A_{e_k,e_l}(s_1,s_2)$ in Definition \ref{Dcondensed} is also abbreviated as $A=(A^1,\dots, A^5)v$, and  $\mu^+_{2kl}$ is abbreviated as $\mu$.  For $\tilde x=(x,y,s_1,s_2,s_3,t)$, we have 
\[
\mu(\tilde x)=(x+A^1v,x+(1-t)s_3u+A^2v, y-(1-t)s_3u+A^3v,x-ts_3u+A^4v, y+ts_3u+A^5v). 
\] Straightforward computation shows 
\[
r\circ \pi_P\circ \mu(\tilde x)=\frac{1}{12}\left(7x+5y+\rho\bigl(2c_3-2c_1+\frac{3}{2}c_5-\frac{3}{2}c_4\bigr)u+2(A^1+A^2+A^3)v+3(A^4+A^5)v\right).
\] 
We denote right hand side of this equation by $w$. For simplicity, we move the fiber of $\nu$ over $\pi_P(\mu(\tilde x))$ by the parallel transport which sends its center to $0$. By this move, $\mu(\tilde x)$ is sent to to 
\[
\mu(\tilde x)-e_P(\mu(\tilde x))=(p,q,-p-q, r,-r)
\] 
where 
\[
\begin{split}
p &=\frac{1}{3}(x-y)+\frac{\rho}{6}(2c_1+3c_2+c_3)u+\frac{1}{3}(2A^1-A^2-A^3)v\\
q &=\frac{1}{3}(x-y)+\frac{\rho}{6}(c_3-c_1)u+(1-t)s_3u+\frac{1}{3}(-A^1+2A^2-A^3)v\\
r&=\frac{1}{2}(x-y)+\frac{\rho}{4}(c_4+c_5)u-ts_3u+\frac{1}{2}(A^4-A^5)v\\
\end{split}
\] The fiber over $\pi_P$ is a disk of radius $\epsilon_P$. It is enough to show that there exists a point $\tilde x$ such that $p'=p$, $q'=q$, $r'=r$  for a given point $(p',q',r')$ with $|(p',q',r')|\leq \epsilon_P$, and the combination of such a point and numbers $k,l$ is unique unless $s_1$ or $s_2=0$. We fix $w, p', q',r'$ and suppose $(p',q',r')=(p,q,r)$.  By using the formula for $r$, we eliminate $x-y$ in the formulas of $p,q$. We have
\[
\begin{split}
p'&=\frac{1}{3}(2r'+(2ts_3+\frac{\rho}{2}(2c_1+3c_2+c_3-c_4-c_5))u+(2A^1-A^2-A^3-A^4+A^5)v),\\
q'&=\frac{1}{3}(2r'+((3-t)s_3+\frac{\rho}{2}(c_3-c_1-c_4-c_5))u+(-A^1+2A^2-A^3-A^4+A^5)v).
\end{split}
\]
By these formulas, we have
\[
\begin{split}
s_3&=\frac{1}{4}(2p'_1+2q'_1-4r'_1+\rho(c_4+c_5-c_2-c)),\\
t&=\frac{6p'_1-4r'_1+\rho(c_4+c_5-2c_1-3c_2-3c_3)}{2p'_1+4q'_1-4r'_1+\rho (c_4+c_5-c_2-c_3),}
\end{split}
\]
where the subscript $1$ means the first coordinate. The conditions on $c_i$ and $(p',q',r')$ ensure $s_3>0$ and $0<t<1$. We consider the  last terms of the above formulas for $p',q'$. For $(k,l)=(1,2), (1,3),$ and $(2,3)$, we have
\[
\begin{split}
(2A^1-A^2&-A^3-A^4+A^5, -A^1+2A^2-A^3-A^4+A^5)\\
&=(4s_1-2s_2,-2s_1+4s_2), \quad 
 (4s_1-2s_2,-2s_1-2s_2), \ \text{ and }\ (-2s_1-2s_2, 4s_1-2s_2),
\end{split}
\]
respectively. When $s_1, s_2\geq 0$ vary, this point runs through a domain $D_{kl}$. It is easy to see that $\cup_{kl}D_{kl}=\RR^2$, $\partial D_{kl}$ corresponds to $s_1$ or $s_2=0$, and $D_{kl}\cap D_{k',l'}\subset \partial D_{kl} \cap \partial D_{k',l'} $ if $(k,l)\not=(k',l')$. So, the coefficients of $v$ can take any value.  When we fix $(p',q',r')$, $x-y$ is fixed but $7x+5y$ can take any value, so we can set $w$ freely. Thus, we have proved the claim.  
\end{proof}
\subsection{Non-triviality of the differential}\label{SSnon-triviality}
To prove non-triviality of the differential $d_3$, we need to prove that a generator of $\EE^{-2,1}_2$ persists to $E_3$-page. For this, we compute $d_2:\EE_2^{-4,2}\to \CEE_2^{-2,1}$. The module $\EE^{-4,2}_2$ is generated by   $g_{13}g_{24}$.  We actually consider the spectral sequence $tr_1\CEE$. A cycle which represents $g_{13}g_{24}$ is a linear combination of chains in the Thom spaces of the following three graphs (and their subgraphs).  
\[
G_5=(1,3)(2,4),\qquad G_6=(1,3)(1,4),\qquad G_7=(1,4)(2,4)
\]
The computation of $d_2$ is similar to those done in section \ref{Sch2} and previous subsections. One difference is that we need to deal with the 3-term relation. To make the computation easier, we modify the definition of chains.
\begin{defi}\label{D(e,-)-cont}
Let $f:X\to \RR^8$ be $G$-condensed map and $e=(\alpha,\beta)$ be a bridge of $G$.
In this subsection, we call the $e$-contraction given in Definition \ref{Dcondensed} the {\em $(e,+)$-contraction}. The {\em $(e,-)$-contraction} of $f$ for $G$ is a version of $e$-contraction whose contracting direction is reversed. We add $-sv$ (resp. $sv$) to the $i$-th component if $i\sim_{\partial_eG} \alpha$ (resp. $i\sim_{\partial_eG}\beta$).  
\end{defi} 
\begin{exa}\label{E(e,-)-cont}
Put $f=f_{G_5}, e=(1,3)$. the $(e,-)$-contraction of $f$ (for $G_5$) is given by  
$(x,y,s)\mapsto (x-sv,y,x+sv,y)$.
\end{exa}
\begin{defi}\label{Dchain_3term}
For $G=G_5,G_6,$ and $G_7$, set $f=f_G$ and $\{e_1<e_2\}=E(G)$.  Let $f^{\eps}_j$ be the $(e_j,\eps)$-contraction of $f$. Set
\[
c'(G)=f(w_0)-f^{\pm}_1(w_1)+f^\pm_2(w_1).
\]
Here, $f^{\pm}_j$ denotes the average as before. We consider the chain
\[
C'=-c'(G_5)+c'(G_6)+c'(G_7).
\]
\end{defi}
Clearly, we have $D(C')=0$.\\
We shall construct a bounding chain of $\delta C'$. We define two chain $c'(G_5,G_6,1)$ and $c'(G_5,G_7,3)$ satisfying
\[
Dc'(G_5,G_6,1)=\delta_1c'(G_6)-\delta_1c'(G_5), \qquad Dc'(G_5,G_7,1)=\delta_1c'(G_7)-\delta_1c'(G_5),
\]
respectively. The definition is similar to that of $c(G,H,i)$ with taking $(e,\eps)$-contractions into consideration and we omit details. We shall construct a bounding chain of  
$\delta_2C'$. Put $f_1=f_{G_5}, f_2=f_{G_6},$ and $f_3=f_{G_7}$. Below, we denote edges of all involved graphs with two edges by the same notation $e_1<e_2$. Since $\delta_k$ which appears here does not permute edges, this does not cause confusion. Let $\lambda_{ij}^\eps$ be the straight homotopy from $(e_j,\eps)$-contraction of $f_i$ for $G_i$ to $(e_j,\eps)$-contraction of $f_i$ for $\delta_2G_i$, 
$\psi$ the straight homotopy  from $f_1$ to  $f_2$, 
$\phi$ the straight homotopy  from  $f_1$ to  $f_3$, $\psi_j^{\eps}$ $(e_j,\eps)$-contraction of $\psi$ for $\delta_2G_6$, $\phi_j^\eps$ $(e_j,\eps)$-contraction of $\phi$ for $\delta_2G_7$. Let $G_8\in\GG(\delta_2[5])$ be the graph with $E(G_8)=\{(1,\{23\}),(1,4),(\{23\},4)\}$. We set
\[
c(G_5,G_6,G_7,2)=f_1(w_0)+(\psi+\phi)(w_{01})+\sum_{j=1,2}(\psi^\pm_j+\phi^\pm_j+\lambda^\pm_{1j}-\lambda^\pm_{2j}-\lambda^\pm_{3j})(w_{11})
\]
where the pushforward by $f_1$, (resp. $\psi$, $\phi$, $\psi^\eps_j$, $\phi^\pm_j$) belongs to $\bar C_*(G_8)$, (resp. $\bar C_*(\delta_2G_6)$, $\bar C_*(\delta_2G_7)$, $\bar C_*(\delta_2\partial_jG_6)$, $\bar C_*(\delta_2\partial_jG_7)$). This is well-defined as $f_1$ is also $\delta_2G_6$- and $\delta_2G_7$-condensed. 
\begin{lem}\label{L3term}
We have
$Dc(G_5,G_6,G_7,2)=\delta_2C'$. 
If we set $\Gamma'=c(G_5,G_6,1)+c(G_5,G_7,3-)-c(G_5,G_6,G_7,2)$, we have $D\Gamma'=-\delta C'$.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Since $\delta_2\partial_1G_6=\delta_2\partial_2G_7$, the pushforwards by $\psi^\eps_1$ and $\phi^\eps_2$ belongs to the same summand,  and we see $\psi^\pm_1|_{t=0}=\phi^\mp_2|_{t=0}$, where $t$ is the variable for straight homotopy. (This twist of sign is the reason why we introduce $(e,-)$-contraction.) These observations and elementary computation imply  first equation. Second one follows from first one. 
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}\label{Pd2_ch3}
Let $\Gamma'$ be the chain defined in Lemma \ref{L3term}. $\delta\Gamma'$ is  null-homologous in the chain complex $(tr_1\mathbb{T}, D)=(tr_1\CEE_0,d_0)$. In particular, $d_2(g_{13}g_{24})=0$ in $\EE_2$. 
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
By degree reason, we may consider $\bar \EE_r$ instead of $\EE_r$. Among the terms of $\delta\Gamma'$,  $\delta_2(c'(G_5,G_6,1))$ only contains non-zero terms. Each of $(e,+)$- and $(e,-)$-contractions of this term forms a cycle and we can easily see their classes cancel with each other similarly to the proof of Lemmas \ref{Lfundcyc} and \ref{Lfundcyc_ch3}. Latter part easily follows from former one and Lemma \ref{Lspeqseq}.
\end{proof}
In view of Lemma \ref{Lfundcyc_ch3} and Proposition  \ref{Pd2_ch3}, the proof of the following theorem is completely similar to Theorem \ref{Tdifferential}.
\begin{thm}\label{Tch3}
In dimension $d=2$ and over a field of  characteristic $3$,  the  element $[-c(G_1)+c(G_2)+c(G_3)+c(G_4)]\in tr_1\CEE^{-3,5}_1$ lifts to an element $ g\in \EE^{-3,5}_1$ which persists up to $\EE^{-3,5}_3$ and satisfies $d_3(g)\not=0$.
\hfill \qedsymbol
\end{thm}

\section{Absolute non-formality in characteristic 2}\label{Sabsolute}
In this section, we prove Corollary \ref{Cnon-formality_ch2}. We assume that $\kk$ is a field of characteristic $2$ and $d=2$. Let $\ASS_\infty$ denote the cellular chain operad of Stasheff's associahedral operad. Precisely speaking,  
$\ASS_\infty$ is generated by a set $\{\, \mu_k\in \ASS_\infty(k) \, \}_{k\geq 2}$ (\ $|\mu_k|=k-2$\ )  with partial compositions. The differential is given by the following formula:
\[
d\mu _k=\sum_{
l,p,q}\,\mu_l\circ_{p+1}\mu_q.
\]
where $l,p,q$ runs through the range $l, q\geq 2, 0\leq p\leq l-1,$ and $l+q=k+1$ .
\begin{defi}\label{Dhochschild}
For a $\kk$-vector space $U$, $U^\vee$ denotes its linear dual. Let $f:\ASS_\infty\to \oper$ be a map of chain operads. Let $\mu'_l\in \oper(l)$ be the image of $\mu_l\in\ASS_\infty(l)$ by $f$. we define a linear map $(-\circ_i\mu_l):\oper(m)^\vee\to \oper(m-l+1)^\vee$ for integers $m\geq l$ and $1\leq i\leq l$ as the following composition
\[
\xymatrix{\oper(m)^\vee\ar[r]^{(-\circ_i-)^\vee\qquad \qquad}& (\oper(l)\otimes\oper(m-l+1))^\vee\ar[r]& \oper(m-l+1)^\vee }
\] 
where the right arrow is the evaluation of the first factor on  $\mu'_l$. We also define $(\mu_l\circ_i-): \oper(m)^\vee\to \oper(m-l+1)^\vee$ for integers $m\geq l$ and $1\leq i\leq m-l+1$ similarly. 
We define a chain complex  $(\Hoch\oper, \tilde d)$ called \textit{Hochschild complex of $\oper$},  as follows.
Set $\Hoch^{-p,q}\oper=(\oper (p)_q)^\vee$.  The  differential $\tilde d$ is given as a map
\[
\tilde d =d+\delta : \underset{q-p=k}{\bigoplus}\Hoch^{-p,q} \oper \longrightarrow \underset{q-p=k+1}{\bigoplus}\Hoch^{-p,q} \oper.
\] 
Here $d$ is the internal (original) differential on $\oper(p)^\vee$ and $\delta$ is given by the  formula $\delta (x)=\sum_{l\geq  2}\mu_l* x$
where
\[
\mu_l*x=x\circ_1\mu_l+x\circ_l\mu_l+\sum_{i=1}^{p-l+1}\mu_l\circ_ix
\]
for $x\in \oper (p)^\vee$. We define a  spectral sequence $E^{-p,q}_r(\oper)$ by filtering $(\Hoch\oper, \tilde d)$ by the arity $p$.
\end{defi}
We call a map $f:\oper\to \aoper$ of chain operads  a {\em weak-equivalence} if it induces a quasi-isomorphism $\oper(p)\to \aoper(p)$ for each $p$. 
The following lemma is clear.
\begin{lem}\label{Lspec_natural}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Let $\ASS_\infty\to \oper$ and $\ASS_\infty\to \oper'$ be two maps of operads and $f:\oper\to \oper'$ a weak equivalence compatible with the maps from $\ASS_\infty$. Then $f$ induces an isomorphism $E_r(\oper)\cong E_r(\oper')$ compatible with the differentials for $r\geq 1$.
\item Let $\ASS_\infty\to C_*(\KK_2)$ be the composition $\ASS_\infty\to \ASS\to C_*(\KK_2)$ of the fixed maps (see Definition \ref{DKontsevich}). The spectral sequence $\{E_r(C_*(\KK_2))\}$ is isomorphic to $\{\EE_r\}$ (see Definition \ref{Dspecseq}). \hfill\qedsymbol
\end{enumerate}
\end{lem}
The following lemma is easily obtained by unwinding the definition of the spectral sequence $E_r(\oper)$.
\begin{lem}\label{Lspec_diff_description}
Let $\ASS_\infty\to \oper$ be a map of operads. 
\begin{enumerate}
\item $d_1([x])=[\mu_2*x]$ for an element $[x]\in E^{-p,q}_1(\oper)$ represented by $x\in \oper(p)^\vee_q$.
\item For an element $[x]\in E^{-p,q}_2(\oper)$ represented by $x\in \oper(p)^\vee_q$, we  can take an element \\$y\in \oper(p-1)_{q-1}^\vee$ with $dy=\mu_2*x$. We have $d_2[x]=[\mu_2*y+\mu_3*x]$.\hfill \qedsymbol
\end{enumerate}
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary \ref{Cnon-formality_ch2}]
Let $\ASS_\infty\to C_*(\KK_2)$ be the map given in Lemma \ref{Lspec_natural}. We use the projective model structure on the category of planar chain operads (see e.g. \cite{moriya}). We take a cofibrant replacement $\ASS_\infty\rightarrowtail \oper\stackrel{\sim}{\twoheadrightarrow} C_*(\KK)$. 
Suppose that $C_*(\KK_2)$ is formal. By this assumption, we  can take a weak equivalence of operads $\oper\to H_*(\KK)$. By considering the composition $f:\ASS_\infty\to \oper\to H_*(\KK)$, we obtain an isomorphism of spectral sequences $E_r(\oper)\cong E_r(H_*(\KK))$. Let $\mu'_l\in H_{l-2}(\KK_2(l))$ be the image of $\mu_l$ by $f$. By definition of $\ASS_\infty$, we see 
\[
\mu'_2\circ_1\mu'_3+\mu'_2\circ_2\mu'_3+\mu'_3\circ_1\mu'_2+\mu'_3\circ_2\mu'_2+\mu'_3\circ_3\mu'_2=d\mu'_4=0.
\]
This means that $\mu'_3$ is a cycle for the differential of the unnormalized complex of  the cosimplicial vector space associated to the fixed map $\ASS\to H_*(\KK)$. By easy (and well-known) computation, this differential is a monomorphism on $H_1(\KK(3))$, so we have $\mu'_3=0$. This observation and Lemma \ref{Lspec_diff_description} imply $d_2=0$ for $E_2(H_*(\KK))$. Since $E_r(\oper)$ is isomorphic to $E_r(C_*(\KK))\cong \EE_r$, this vanishing of differential contradicts to Theorem \ref{Tdifferential}.
\end{proof}
