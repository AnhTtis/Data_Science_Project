A belief transition in the form $q\colon X\otimes A\to X\otimes B$ can be construed as predicting the effects $\out q(x,a)\colon B$ of current beliefs $x$ and some observed causes $a$, and moreover updating the beliefs to $\sta q(x,a)\colon X$. The explanations of such belief updates are verified by testing whether they apply not only to the current beliefs but also to the updates. An explanation $\Upsilon$ is therefor required to correctly predict not only the effects $\out q(x,a) = \out{\uev{\Upsilon x}}a$ but also the updates $\Upsilon\big(\sta q(x,a)\big) = \sta{\uev{\Upsilon x}} a$ which will correctly predict future effects. If it fails to explain some future observations or the beliefs leading to the predictions, it is considered \emph{falsified}, and a better explanation is sought. This is a crude common denominator of various views of the process of scientific testing. Our claim is that explanations can, \emph{in theory}, be constructed to never fail such tests but always explain all updated beliefs.
